Project 0 — Becoming Friends with Your Camera

The goal of this project is to get some intuitive understanding of the somewhat subtle relationship between perspective, focal length/zoom, and the center of projection.

Part 1: Selfie — The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

This part is all about exploring the relationship between distance and aesthetics in phone photography. Think about a typical close-up selfie you've taken. The image is often distorted: your nose seems larger, your eyes might look a bit strange, and your face appears wider than it is in real life. This is a classic example of perspective distortion, a common issue with close-up phone photos. Now, let's try something different. Step back a few feet from your subject, then use your phone's zoom to bring their face back to the same size it was in the close-up shot. Take another picture. You'll likely notice that this new portrait looks much better—the proportions are more natural, and the face appears more three-dimensional and realistic.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

This part focuses on how distance and perspective affect architectural photography. The process is a simple experiment in two steps. First, stand far from a building and use your phone's zoom to take a photo. You'll notice the image looks flattened, with the building appearing compressed against its background. Next, walk closer to that building and, without any zoom, take a second photo, getting the building to appear roughly the same size in your frame. This new shot will have a more dynamic and natural perspective, with a stronger sense of depth and scale.

Zoomed from far (tele) — flattened look
Closer without zoom (wide) — deeper look

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom ("Vertigo shot")

This part focuses on the dolly zoom, a classic film effect also known as the "Vertigo shot" after its famous use in a Hitchcock film. It's a surreal visual trick where you physically move the camera in one direction while simultaneously zooming the lens in the opposite direction. This makes the background appear to stretch and warp dramatically, while the main subject stays the same size in the frame.
